About Bangus (Milkfish)

The national fish of the Philippines and top aquaculture species. Found in tropical waters from the Indian Ocean to the central Pacific. Key driver of the Philippine fisheries economy.

  • Detected by FishTrack YOLOv8 at >87% confidence in clear coastal water
  • Spawning season: March–June; peaks April–May in Visayas
  • Diet: algae, diatoms, cyanobacteria (herbivorous)
  • Optimal temp: 25–32 °C; saline 10–32 ppt
Citation: FAO. (2023). Chanos chanos. Cultured Aquatic Species Information Programme. Rome. DA-BFAR. (2023). Philippine Milkfish Production Statistics.

About Lapu-lapu (Tiger Grouper)

A prized reef fish named after the Filipino hero who defeated Magellan in 1521. Critically important for Philippine artisanal fisheries and mariculture. Found on coral reefs of the Coral Triangle.

  • Detected by FishTrack at reef zones; >82% confidence in clear water
  • Spawning: February–April; aggregates on reef edges
  • Diet: small fish, squid, crustaceans; ambush predator
  • Optimal temp: 26–30 °C; high-salinity reef waters
Citation: Ma & Craig. (2018). Epinephelus fuscoguttatus. IUCN Red List of Threatened Species. e.T132753A100540585. Froese & Pauly, FishBase, 2024.

About Galunggong (Round Scad)

The most commonly consumed fish in the Philippines — often called the "poor man's fish." A pelagic schooling species fished by purse seine and ring net in Philippine waters. Critical to food security.

  • Detected by FishTrack in open-water pelagic zones; school detection via sonar
  • Spawning: Year-round; peaks February–May off Visayas
  • Diet: zooplankton, small crustaceans, fish larvae
  • Preferred temp: 24–30 °C; strong diel migration pattern
Citation: Froese, R. & Pauly, D. (2024). Decapterus macrosoma. FishBase. BFAR. (2023). Fisheries Statistics of the Philippines 2022. Quezon City: DA-BFAR.

Radio Technology

RSSI Signal Quality Guide

Excellent (−50 to −70 dBm)

Full signal strength. Ideal for high-frequency telemetry bursts across Philippine coastal waters.

Good (−70 to −90 dBm)

Reliable for standard data rates. Most Coral Triangle buoy deployments fall in this range.

Fair (−90 to −105 dBm)

Occasional packet loss due to wave interference. Reduce spreading factor for better sensitivity.

Poor (<−105 dBm)

High loss rate. Increase Spreading Factor to SF12 or reposition buoy to reduce obstructions.

Philippine Oceanography

Philippine Sea Depth Zones & Species Habitats

Understanding which depth zone each species occupies helps operators configure FishTrack sonar sweep ranges and interpret buoy camera detection data accurately.

0 – 200 m Epipelagic — Sunlight Zone (Coral Triangle)
Bangus — Chanos chanos (1–30 m) Lapu-lapu — E. fuscoguttatus (1–60 m) Galunggong — D. macrosoma (10–170 m) Tambakol — Yellowfin Tuna (0–200 m) Sugpo — P. monodon (0–40 m)

FishTrack sonar operates in this zone. The Philippine Coral Triangle epipelagic layer supports over 1,700 reef fish species and is the primary zone for commercial and municipal fisheries production, contributing over 3.9 million MT annually to the national catch. [DA-BFAR, 2023]

200 – 1,000 m Mesopelagic — Twilight Zone (Philippine Sea)
Deep-water Squid spp. Myctophidae (Lanternfish) Alepisaurus (Lancetfish) Sternoptychidae (Hatchetfish)

Diel vertical migration (DVM) occurs here in Philippine waters — mesopelagic organisms ascend to the epipelagic zone each night to feed. This layer is largely unexplored in Philippine fisheries science. Beyond FishTrack's current sonar operational range. [SEAFDEC/AQD, 2022]

6,000 – 10,540 m Hadal Zone — Philippine Trench (Galathea Depth)
Amphipoda (Deep-sea Crustaceans) Holothuroidea (Sea Cucumbers) Polychaeta (Bristle Worms) Snailfish (Liparidae)

The Philippine Trench reaches 10,540 m — one of the deepest points on Earth, located in the Philippine Sea east of Mindanao. Pressure exceeds 1,000 atmospheres. First explored in 1951 by the Danish vessel Galathea. FishTrack buoys are not designed for these extreme depths. [MBARI Philippine Trench Expedition, 2021; Scripps Institution of Oceanography]

Sources: NOAA Ocean Explorer — Ocean Zones; FishBase species depth profiles (Froese & Pauly, 2024); SEAFDEC Aquaculture Department. (2022). Philippine Waters Marine Resources Assessment. Iloilo: SEAFDEC/AQD. Depth of Philippine Trench from MBARI (2021).

About the Dashboard

What does this do? The System Dashboard gives you a bird's-eye view of your entire LoRa marine sensor mesh network. It shows connected buoy nodes, radio packets counters, and the live activity log, plus a Philippine Marine Education Hub below.

Why is it important? As a central command center, the dashboard helps operators ensure the Base Station is transmitting correctly and that buoy nodes in the Coral Triangle are responding regularly.

Recommended Workflow

  1. Verify LoRa Connection is "Healthy". If Disconnected, check USB cabling to the Pico transceiver.
  2. Ensure the Active Neighbors count matches deployed buoy sensors in Philippine waters.
  3. Inspect the Live System Feed for incoming GPS coordinates or battery alert warnings.

Glossary

Pico Transceiver
The Raspberry Pi Pico hardware module running LoRa firmware connected via USB to the base station server, acting as the radio gateway.
Mesh Neighbors
Buoy nodes that are in direct single-hop radio line-of-sight communication with the Base Station.
BFAR
Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources — the primary Philippine government agency managing fisheries and aquatic resources under the Department of Agriculture.
Coral Triangle
A 6-million km² marine region encompassing the Philippines, Indonesia, Malaysia, Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, and Timor-Leste — the global center of coral reef biodiversity.
